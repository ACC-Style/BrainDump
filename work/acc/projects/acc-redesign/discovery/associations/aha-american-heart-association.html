American Heart Association (heart.org)

Readability:
Score: 35 – Great; Could Benefit from Minor Edits

Breakdown:

Criteria Score Notes
Reading Level 4 (Good) Generally written at an 8th–10th grade level, clear for the general public.
Sentence Length 4 (Good) Sentences mostly range from 15–20 words; a few slightly longer.
Paragraph Density 4 (Good) Most paragraphs are brief (1–3 sentences); easy to scan.
Vocabulary 4 (Good) Mostly simple vocabulary; accessible to a wide audience.
Jargon Usage 4 (Good) Minimal jargon; terms like "AFib" are briefly explained.
Sentence Structure 5 (Excellent) Predominantly active voice; crisp and clear.
Scannability 5 (Excellent) Highly scannable: headings, CTAs, and bullet points aid navigation.
CTA Clarity 5 (Excellent) Calls to action (e.g., "Donate," "Find support") are clear and actionable.

Summary:
Heart.org performs reasonably well for an educated general audience. It offers a mix of technical and accessible vocabulary, and while sentence length and density are moderate, content is largely digestible. The tone is professional, and medical jargon is often defined, especially in patient-facing sections. The layout supports decent scannability, though CTA buttons could be clearer, more action-oriented, but using some great focused text for CTA.

Estimated Visitors: Approximately 3 million monthly visitors.
Estimated Membership Numbers: Over 33 million volunteers and supporters.

Membership Experience

Explanation: The main site offers the majority of its content—including patient education, healthy living resources, donation opportunities, and volunteer programs—freely available to the public. While professional tools and journal access may require membership or payment, these are not part of the main site’s public-facing content.

  • Most of the content is on Proffesional.heart.org so much so the login doesn't show up until the user is on that subdomain.
  • Strong Brand holds the Subs together but the main nav is "Teased" on the main site but the entire nav swaps out when your in that experience.
    Explanation: The site includes information on getting involved through volunteering, donations, and becoming a heart health advocate. Brand Personality

Explanation: The AHA addresses both the general public and professionals, offering resources ranging from patient education to scientific research, reflecting a broad inclusivity.​ The content is designed to be accessible and relevant to a broad audience, including the general public, patients, and caregivers, without requiring specialized medical knowledge.
Explanation: The tone across AHA's platforms is professional yet accessible, aiming to engage a wide audience without compromising on scientific integrity.

    Rationale:

  • Membership & Networking Opportunities: Minimal presence on heart.org. Professional networking largely lives off-site or in conference contexts.

  • Donations & Philanthropy: Highly visible and emphasized, with multiple calls to donate, fundraise, or give in honor of someone.

  • Events: Community events like Heart Walks and Go Red for Women are promoted, but less focus on large-scale scientific events or purchasable events.

  • Scientific Publications / Latest Science: Virtually absent from the main site; most science communication is generalized for lay audiences.

  • Practice Support Resources: Some tools for CPR, emergency response, and guidelines for laypersons exist, but not robust clinical tools unless you know where you are going and then you can find an equivalent to NCDR with Get with the Guideline Registry.

  • Patient-Facing Tools & Content: Heart.org excels in this category, offering rich educational content, risk assessments, and interactive tools.

  • Education: Strong emphasis on public education, and awareness campaigns But limited training courses (like CPR), and no purchasable continuing education or modules for medical professionals. All reference to continuing learning is buried on the main site and is mostly hosted on its E-learning Sub Domain. elearn.heart.org

  • Advocacy: It is on the site but it is very hard to find but does exist.
