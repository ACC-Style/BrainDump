Front End Design at the College

Frontend development continues to expand as a percentage of project coding to the point that some of our products, such as the clinical applications, are 100% frontend code. Over the past year, I have been putting work together to support a massive shift in how we do frontend and, in the long run, how we develop, support, and release our college software solutions. 

The most significant issue I continually see in IT is decentralized coding efforts. Decentralization leads to repetitive solutions, inconsistent behaviors, extended regression testing, bloated packages, complicated deployments, and potential code errors as we try to make security patches or upgrades.  

Focusing on improving how we execute frontend for the college and not just for a project, I have tackled four pillars.  

  1. Unification of design but building for flexibility
  2. Shifting to the CFD (component first development )
  3. Creating documentation that is utilized and not just ignored
  4. Centralizing everything for everyone

The unification of design was the first place I started. "Arches" is the current solution that enforces a Design Opps procedure to streamline requests and deliver a college-wide design system. This design system has two tenants. First, it can replicate its styles influenced by the brand and frameworks from its product space. It now covers QII, ACC, Cardiosmart, and Mobile and can be further scaled. Second is its utility framework that ensures a highly flexible practice to allow countless UI permutations without CSS mutations. Utility styling is also known as "scopeless" styling because you can build out many UI patterns without ever altering the original styles.   

https://assets.acc.org/Arches/Latest/docs/

Shifting to CFD (component first development) embraces an architecture for our frontend products and alters how we develop. CFD, as a pattern, has been around since 2012 with bootstrap. In its infancy, CFD could only capture minor token UI. Still, with the introduction of javascript models like Web Components, React, Vue CFD can now capture business logic, complicated interface interactions, and data mutations. Vue has been my primary focus as it builds on the collective knowledge of the department. Vue has the capabilities to make obsolete Jquery UI, Pager, Knockout, Angular, JqueryMobile, and many other libraries that are currently in use. IT streamlining to a single dependency allows for universal software patching for frontend security issues. 

Currently, we are creating documentation that is utilized and not just ignored. As we shift to CFD, we can use Storybook which allows for documentation around a living singular component with artificial data. This type of documentation gives a single point of Design, Development, and QA. The effort on a single element can be attached to countless projects through version control. Centralized development and QA can give us a feasible path to automated unit testing and regression testing. As new projects come online, utilizing the global-version-controlled components would benefit from standards, consistency, and quality control. Preliminary work done with Storybook around a future product under the ACC.org "Home Page Redesign." Projects could include the transcript, CSTool, GL Search, Coveo Search, and FIT Management.

Vue 2 component for reference
https://acc-style.github.io/Origami/?path=/story/welcome--to-storybook

Vue 3 component for all new work
https://assets.acc.org/papercrane/Latest/docs/

Centralizing everything for everyone. I have conversations GM, Josh G, and Micheal M, and the great work of Ling C on creating a path forward with using assets.acc.org and also moving the newly used submodule features from GIT within current and future products. Submodules empower the component work to be checked in and out from both the centralized-version-control and from projects, these efforts can be kept in sync. The vision for this project is to be controlled by ACC but utilizable by vendors.

Milford Track- Day 4: The Walkout

Morning routines and well-earned stiffness slow us down. Those twelve steps were a challenge on the knees, and we contemplated how difficult the flat 13-mile hike out to the boat would be to take us to our last lodge. Painkillers, stretching, and coffee get us back into the group with poles in our hands and packs on our backs.

At this point, we have all learned that the sand flies are not to be trifled with because their unassuming bites have turned red and extremely itchy. Both Vivian and I woke up trying not to itch at 2 AM, and this area doesn't just have more of these little vampires, but the air is cloudy with them. Slathered with bug dope they still find areas not perfectly covered.

We are driven by them because any pause in movement causes a Pigpen-like swarm to form around you in seconds.

This day starts off 2 miles downhill and then breaks into a rolling, flat terrain. Thankfully, this downhill isn't nearly as challenging as the trail leading up to the lodge. The first break is at 5.5 miles at a boat house and then a second lunch spot at 4.5 miles at Giants Gate Falls, and then the trail wraps up in the 3-mile segment where we will transfer over 17 people at a time every half an hour to our final lodge in Milford Sound.

Most of the trail has minimal inclines up or down, and it consists of crushed rock with few roots and infrequent steps, but it allows us to keep up the speed. Markers pass quickly, and our pace is brisk, driven by the flies and the unchallenging trail.

Dechland, our guide, keeps pace with us for a while, and we talk about our background and then board games. He is a friendly Australian who is quite interesting. He is the most social guide we had on the trip. 5.5 miles blur by, and we approach lunch stop number 1. I guess while in New Zealand eat like a hobbit.

At the boat house, we dash inside after dropping packs because the flies are extra vicious.

We passed Mackay Falls which is a gorgeous emerald-pooled waterfall. It is said to be the prettiest falls in New Zealand and I would hate to pick favorites but this would be one of the best we had seen over our adventures on these islands.

In and out of dense mossed trees, I would not have believed it could get greener from our first day, but everywhere was green leafy, and the ground was a soft sponge-like footing at times. It was a pleasant walk heading in the valley of the pass.

Markers started to blur without a picture taken in between as we crept higher toward thirty. Then we burst onto one of the longest suspension bridges and saw many of our fellow hikes in the mist wash of Nuku-tau-roa/Giants Gate Falls.

The strong breeze and mist of the falls kept most sandflies at bay. I ate the sweets from the lunch collection, a gluten-free coconut chocolate bar, and the inner pieces of my leftover sandwich. Having done the math knowing we would be forced to wait on rounds of boat crossings while being quickly devoured by the bugs I wanted to make sure I was on the 2nd boat. We had met up with Troy and Mary, and I set what felt like a blistering pace. If my math were correct, in the next 3 miles, we would have to overtake six other hikers to make it on the 3 pm boat versus the 3:30. Happily stopping for 4 0person selfies with the mileposts, we got to the ferry house and put our names, in for slots 28-32. After we had a seat secured we then strolled to the last sign saying we had made it to the end of the trail at 34.4 miles at s and fly point.

Because independent hikes get priority for the boat the four of us were the last people called to get on the ship a few moments later.

A jumpy splashing ride later was in Milford warf and busses catried us the last 1km to our lodge.

It was a great hike, and we all felt sore but accomplished. From our lodge we had crystal clear views of the mountains surrounding the Sound.

That night we had a chance to invite out guides to eat with us, and we chatted about many topics, but the topic that made Maya, daughter of Troy and Maria, squeamish was all of us taking turns saying the strangest thing we had ever eaten. I went last knowing I could probably trump the others with either black Scorpion or fire ants on a Salad in Cambodia.

All of tried to last in the bar after dinner and chatted with our now new friends but sleep found all of quickly and even though we didn't have a generator that would kill the power we all dropped off one-by-one before 11pm.
