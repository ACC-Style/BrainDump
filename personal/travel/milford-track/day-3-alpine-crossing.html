Day 3 - Milford Track: Alpine Crossing

The days have now fallen into a routine. Get up stretch to push away the stiffness. Pack out lunch in the center lodge and grab breakfast. Head back to our rooms fold everything back into our backpacks and crush our feet back into our hiking boots. 20ish pounds on our back we are out in the trail.

Cloud cover is thick this morning and we have no expectations of rain so we all head out in mass but because of the incline the divides between people quickly expand till my wife and I are walking with out signs of other hikers.

A good hour in still in the woods we have gained some elevation but we know the worst of it is too come. We drop our pack at the next huts and toilets and are greatest buy glimpses of kea an alpine parrot. The birds are talkative and very loud.

Back on the trail we start the Alpine crossing. 11 original switch backs that are roughly a 1/3 km long and because of a rock slide a bonus 4 a, b, c &d of steep short switch backs. Climbing 600m wefeel like we are in Jurassic Park as the parrots continue to sqwak and chatter in the fog-draped trees and moss.

When we get past halfway through the switchbacks We have reached the Alpine division and shadowy glimpse of the parrots haunt us obscured by the the ever thickening clouds. We leave behind the trees giving way to large leafy plants wildflowers and spikey grass. Looking up we can see colorful blurs within the mist of our fellow hikers as we grow close on mountain side as the switch backs cross above and below each other.

The trail finally does fold back on itself and we are on the ridge. Cloud strong winds great us as we continue to climate and drop on the grass topped Mckinnon pass. A monument to the adventure that mapped this route up over the pass. cooling sweat forces us to dawn warmer cloths. Fleece, gloves and hats help us hold onto some warmth as we wander through low visibility fog. At this point I wonder if lugging my 4 lb camera in this trail will even be worth it.

We quickly come pass a sign that I assume was supposed to fill us with hope that it is 30 minutes to the shelter where we are taking a lunch break. But thirty minutes on spent legs, no visibility, and a rocky incline depress us as it is too far away to be a comfort. Multiple cursing sessions later and more then 30 minutes we find ourselves enter the shelter which to me feels like a sauna.

Quickly stripping away the packs and layers we find likely in the back of the pack as some are already gearing back up and heading out. I tell my wife we need to pause here for 30-45 minutes because all the incline we just took will be decline as soon as we leave the shelter and we should let ourselves take in some calories and rest.

At the Alpine crossing after lunch we fill our water packs and get ready to head back out not excited to make the crossing without a view we are slow to get moving. Luckily we were delayed because we were teased with a burlesque show if thining clouds revealing views of the lush Valley we had left behind and mountain tops in our heading direction. By the time we were really heading down the trail the passing had stripped off most of its clouds and sun dazzled in the blue skies. The trail down was steep rocky but was manageable on rested legs. The thin trail hugged a cliff face giving remarkable views of expansion of green grass giving away to trees far bellow all protected on all sides by guarding mountains.

We pass my marker 17 and are in good spirits we are making good time and have crossed 7.5 miles of our 9.5 day and we are in good spirits. Passing over an avalanche field of rocks stream we tuck ourselves back into the tree line saying good bye to the baking sun but also of stunning views. Micheal one of our guides informs us that we are 2 hours from the lodge we thought we miss heard him because an hour a mile doesn't sound right. In the shadow of the trees the trail turns difficult deep steps down balancing on rock edges make the trail challenge. Our watches run out off power and we have no idea how far we have gone as we drop 100s of meters as we cross this last section before the end of our day. The trail turns so steep that a metal staircase was in stalled over looking a cascade of water and waterfalls off of the left. Loosing heart that we haven't seen a trail marker we wonder if we have just gotten to a crawl. I try my best to keep spirits up as Vivian fights for every step. Finally we pass marker 19 that both gives us hope and crushes our mood because we have a mile or more before we are done for the day. I have slowed down pointing out the jutting rock ends that are smaller step downs for Vivian and besides the small bursts of encouragements that we are doing great and that she is amazing we hike in mostly silence. I cheer her on as we pass the 20th mile marker and we know the turn off to the lodge is some where before the 21st marker. Shuffling the last deep trail we discuss that we most likely won't add on the trail to the waterfall even though we have miraculously gotten in before the time cut off to add in the extra 1.5 hour hike.

Like zombies we head into the lodge snacks and drink are waiting for us as we wait for our tour of the lodge and our room designation. Our room is on the second floor and the twelve steps seem like an ask too much but we make it. In the room I collapse on the floor and tear off my boots just to free of them. Laying on the floor I let all my joints decompress as I groan the challenge of the day. Some combination of FOMO and finding another wind I decide I'm going to the waterfall and adding more distance into my day and I am shocked when Vivian agrees to do it also. The trail to the Sutherland Falls says it is 45 minutes to the tallest waterfall in New Zealand and we head out. Thankfully the first third of the hike is mostly flat and smooth. We question our sanity when the trail starts a dramatic change of up and deep stairs. We curse ourselves as we add extra onto an already exhausting day. I am now determined to see the falls and my will power seems to drag my wife along too. The last third is a nice repreave of flat-ish hiking. A sign gives us false hope that states we are now as far from the base of the falls as it is tall. 580m of rock terrain await our passage till we reach the falls. We are the last of the hikers to make the falls and as we get wet from the showering spray from the falls last drop of 280m we are pretty pleased we made it. Declan our sweaper guide moves us on saying we need to head back. Some how we find a solidly good pace back to the lodge and return under 50 minutes. Now closer to 12 miles for the day we permanently say good bye to out books for the day.

Evening rituals of shower, change into lodge cloths, washing hiking cloths and using the dryroom finally gets us back into the common lodge where we are treated to good company, stories of the day from others and a good meal of salad ribeye steaks and desert. Clouds move in so even if we could have lasted till darkness there wouldn't have been stars. Well earned sleep finds us quickly and we don't even notice the generators cutting out at 10pm.
