Edited — Day 5 - A sound of a thousand waterfalls

Milford Track - Day 5: A Sound of a Thousand Waterfalls

We had shipped a change of clothes and shoes ahead to the last lodge, so in the morning, we were thankful not to have to wear trail-washed hiking clothes. Our feet thanked us for not having to be crammed back into boots.

After our usual routine of breakfast and packing lunch, we took a bus to our cruise of Milford Sound. Heading to the bus, we could barely see the mountains of the sound, and a steady misting rain had rolled in, obscuring what last night was a mile of view into the open waters of the sound. Many of us were disappointed about the loss of visibility and a rainy last day, but all were thankful for having excellent weather during the hiking days.

We all settled into the boat, thinking this would be a wasted hour on a boat where nothing interesting would be seen. As soon as we had passed the first half a kilometer into the water, the cliff sides turned into steep stone, and with that, there was nowhere for the rain to go without becoming waterfalls. Any rock face close enough to pierce the curtains of mist and clouds was adorned with lacy draped waterfalls. Milford Sound has three permanent waterfalls, but on rainy days, there were close to a hundred streaming, cascading, and falling waters all across the Sound.

Many of us geared for rain stood in the elements in awe of the spectacle that Milford Sound transforms into during the rain.

The obscured view in any distance makes the stone faces seem impossibly tall. The tops of the stone walls kiss the clouds, and some of the falls seem to originate in the heavens. Three hundred meters is the height of the giant sentinels of the sound, and supposedly, the depth is mirrored in the water.

Because of the depth of the water, the boat at the permanent waterfalls can push its nose into the waterfall itself. When the bow dipped into the sheet of waterfalls, a new rumble was added to the boat, reverberating the steel bones. The water intensified in the air by magnitudes. A few seconds, even at the back of the boat, was enough to allow water to pour off you from every angle.

The captain of the vessel commented that getting wet from falls could keep you young; staying the skipper is close to ninety, and the last captain was 120 before he handed over the boat to him.

Most of us looked like drowned cats as we pulled back into the docks, but all of us were happy to see the magic that Milford could provide. Back, we started our 4-hour ride home. Through raindrop-bedazzled windows, the valley leaving the sound, the temperate rainforest seemed to rejoice and revel in the rain. Like the sound, the mountain edges of the valley wore their jeweled waterfall. A few steep switchbacks on the bus, and we are at the tunnel leaving the Milford area. Close to a kilometer of a dark and lightly flooded tunnel takes us through the highest point of the road.

Exiting the other side, the sky splits open, and the sky shows patches of blue sky.

We all are satisfied, and we doze while heading back to Queenstown. We had done one of the prettiest walks in the world, and we were happy we did it.
