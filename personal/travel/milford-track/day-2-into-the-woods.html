Day 2 - Into the Woods

Day 2- Milford track: into the woods

Early morning starts at the first lodge. Strangers shuffle into the common area as rain falls outside. Tables fill, and the gap of strangers begins to wane as we chat and learn about each other. Buffets of sandwich fixings scatter two tables, and we all take turns hobbling together our lunches with combos of vegetables, including beetroot, carrot, and cucumber slices with meat cheeses and mayo-based salads. We thought wraps would travel best, but we were wrong a few hours later as we washed our hands in a stream to get our hands clean. 

Still drizzling, we started in mass to our day of travel. Raincoats, rain pants, and backpack slicks on almost certainly scared away the rain because as we started, the rain fizzled out with a glimmer of sun breaking off into the distance. Across the first bridge, less than a kilometer, we are officially on our way, hiking the Milford track, and the sun greets us officially for the day. 

Our guides gave us a tiny navigational bit of advice: if the river we just crossed over is ever on our left, we are making our day longer, and we need to turn around. With the Clinton River now our navigational guide for the day, we headed out. The trail is softly rolling with no real incline gain. The flat trails flirt with the river, diving in and out of the woods all morning. Everyone feeling strong, we stayed together in a loose line, with the fastest of us stretching way, and the mass started to thin, so only a few hikers were in view at a time. 

The mile markers quickly became a game as we took selfies with all we could find. The first extra trail along our path came into view as we saw a pile of backpacks, so the guides knew we didn't leave someone behind. We headed toward what I kept called the watershed, but it was a two-minute walk to the wetlands. From the end, it was a circle of wooden paths that broke out of the trees into bushes of blooming Manuka. 

Lush green leaves and newly sprouted Brown fiddle heads spring from bouquets of ferns on both sides of the track. Rocky beaches broke out on the right, edged by the Emerald-tinted Clinton River. 

At mile 6, we say goodbye to the Clinton River, and the track gains more sweeping inclines. By mile 8, we have caught up with our guides and fellow hikers tucking into their sandwiches. At the hut, our guides hand out liquid beverages in mugs: coffee, tea, Milo hot chocolate, and raro, a Gatorade-like powder that, when mixed into the water, makes a tang like orange sugar water. 

After a rest and bathroom break in some of the nicest maintained outhouses I have ever been in because there was supplied TP, we were back on the trail for our final three miles. 

Shortly after restarting the trail, we departed the shelter of the trees, and we entered a spectacular hallway of mountain cliffs gilded with silver-threaded waterfalls. 

Small lakes fed by the falling water scattered over the next few miles. Parts of the trail have 4-foot tall makers with arrows pointing to another post only six feet away. Luckily, a guide passed by and explained that this entire zone of the hike could be submerged by flood waters when it rains hard, and earlier that year, he had passed through with water up to his thigh. Grateful for skipping that variation of the hike, we all thanked the Blues skies and the sun even though it was slowly baking us. A mile from the lodge was our last Lake, where we were encouraged to cool off and dip. Vivian, soaking her feet in the ice water, was happy to record my experience of getting into the water. Cold and clear water after the shock wore off was an excellent salve for sore shoulders and tired feet. 

After drying off, we headed onto the last leg of the day. The end mile ruined what would have been a long but easy walk because we were barred from the end by an avalanche fall. Deep steps and bolder scrambles quickly added another layer of sweetness and a few curses. Thirty minutes later, we found ourselves at the gates of Pamelo Lodge. 

After showers and stretches and putting clean lodge clothes on, we joined our fellow trekking family for drinks and stunning views of the mountains. I was too lazy and tired to go back to my room to retrieve my bug repellent, so I continuously killed the sandflies that were trying to extract a liter of blood. This was the greatest mistake of the hike because I discovered days later that the inoculous bites turned maddeningly itchy spots 2 days later. 

Good conversations sprung up around our table as they served a three-course meal and ended with a Creme Brulee

A small briefing of the day ahead and the days expenditure drove most of us back to our rooms, and we all probably fell asleep before the generators turned off at 10 PM.
