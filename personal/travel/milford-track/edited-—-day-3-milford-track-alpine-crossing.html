Day 3 - Milford Track: Alpine Crossing

The days have now settled into a routine. We wake up and stretch to shake off the stiffness. We prepare our lunch at the central lodge and have breakfast. Then, we return to our rooms, pack everything into our backpacks, and put on our hiking boots. With around 20 pounds on our backs, we hit the trail.

This morning, the sky is heavily clouded, but we don't anticipate any rain, so we all set out together. However, due to the steep terrain, the gaps between hikers quickly widen, and my wife and I soon find ourselves walking alone.

About an hour into the hike, still in the woods and gaining some elevation, we drop our packs at the next huts and toilets. Here, we catch glimpses of Kea, an alpine parrot. These birds are pretty talkative and loud.

Back on the trail, we begin the Alpine crossing. There are 11 original switchbacks, each about a third of a kilometer long. Additionally, due to a rock slide, there are four extra switchbacks labeled a, b, c, and d, which are steep and short. As we ascend 600 meters, the parrots continue to squawk and chatter in the fog-covered trees and moss, creating an almost Jurassic Park-like atmosphere.

Once we pass the halfway point of the switchbacks, we enter the Alpine region. Shadowy glimpses of the parrots still haunt us, obscured by the thickening clouds. We leave the trees behind, giving way to large leafy plants, wildflowers, and spiky grass. Looking up, we can see colorful blurs within the mist of fellow hikers as the switchbacks crisscross above and below each other.

Finally, the trail loops back on itself, and we find ourselves on the ridge. Strong winds and clouds greet us as we continue to climb and descend on the grass-covered Mckinnon Pass. A monument commemorates the adventurers who mapped this route over the pass. Cooling sweat forces us to dawn warmer clothes – fleece, gloves, and hats – as we trek through low-visibility fog. At this point, I begin to question whether lugging my 4 lb camera on this trail will be worthwhile.

We soon pass a sign indicating that it's a 30-minute walk to the shelter where we plan to take a lunch break. However, after more than 30 minutes of challenging hiking on tired legs, no visibility, and rocky terrain, we finally reached the shelter. To me, it feels like a sauna.

Quickly stripping away the packs and layers we find likely in the back of the pack, as some are already gearing back up and heading out. I tell my wife we need to pause here for 30-45 minutes because all the incline we just took will decline as soon as we leave the shelter, and we should let ourselves take in some calories and rest. 

At the Alpine crossing, after lunch we fill our water packs and get ready to head back out. Not excited to make the crossing without a view, we are slow to get moving. Luckily, we were delayed because we were teased with a burlesque show of thining clouds revealing views of the lush Valley we had left behind and mountain tops in our heading direction. By the time we were really heading down the trail, the passing had stripped off most of its clouds, and the sun dazzled in the blue skies. The trail down was steep and rocky but was manageable on rested legs. The thin trail hugged a cliff face, giving remarkable views of the expansion of green grass, giving away to trees far below, all protected on all sides by guarding mountains. 

We pass marker 17, and our spirits are high. We are making good time and have completed 7.5 miles of our 9.5-mile journey for the day. As we cross an avalanche field of rocks and a stream, we re-enter the tree line, saying goodbye to the baking sun and the stunning views. Michael, one of our guides, informs us that we are 2 hours from the lodge. We initially think we misheard him because an hour per mile doesn't sound right. In the shadow of the trees, the trail becomes challenging with deep steps down and balancing on rock edges. Our watches run out of power, and we have no idea how far we've gone as we descend hundreds of meters in this final section before the end of our day. The trail becomes so steep that a metal staircase has been installed, overlooking a cascade of water and waterfalls to the left. We lose heart as we haven't seen a trail marker, wondering if we're making progress. I do my best to keep spirits up as Vivian struggles with each step. Finally, we pass marker 19, which gives us hope but also dampens our mood because we still have a mile or more to go before we're done for the day. I slow down, pointing out the smaller step-downs for Vivian. Aside from sporadic words of encouragement and acknowledgment of her strength, we hike mostly in silence. I cheer her on as we pass the 20th-mile marker, knowing the turnoff to the lodge is somewhere before the 21st marker. Descending the final stretch of the trail, we discuss that we probably won't add the trail to the waterfall, even though we miraculously made it in time to add the extra 1.5-hour hike.

Like zombies, we reach the lodge, where snacks and drinks await us as we wait for our lodge tour and room assignment. Our room is on the second floor, and the twelve steps feel daunting, but we manage. Inside the room, I collapse on the floor and quickly remove my boots to free my tired feet. Lying on the floor, I let all my joints decompress, groaning from the day's challenges. Some combination of FOMO and newfound energy drives me to decide to visit the waterfall, and I'm surprised when Vivian agrees. The trail to Sutherland Falls indicates a 45-minute hike to New Zealand's tallest waterfall, and we set out. Fortunately, the first third of the hike is mostly flat and smooth. Our sanity is questioned as the trail transitions into a series of steep stairs. We curse ourselves for adding more to an already exhausting day. Determination fuels us as we are determined to see the falls, and my willpower drags my wife along, too. The final third offers a reprieve of relatively flat hiking. A sign gives us false hope, stating that we are now as far from the base of the falls as it is tall – 580 meters of rocky terrain await our passage to reach the falls. We are the last hikers to make it to the falls, and as we get wet from the spray of the final 280-meter drop, we feel satisfied that we made it. Declan, our sweeper guide, urges us to head back. Somehow, we find a solid pace on the return journey and return to the lodge in under 50 minutes. With our day's distance now closer to 12 miles, we bid farewell to our boots for the day.

In the evening, we follow our customary routine of showering, changing into lodge clothing, washing our hiking clothes, and using the dryroom. Eventually, we return to the common lodge, where we enjoy good company, listen to stories from others about their day, and savor a meal of salad, ribeye steaks, and dessert. Clouds move in, so there are no stars to be seen even if we had stayed up until darkness. We fall asleep quickly, not even noticing when the generators cut out at 10 pm.
